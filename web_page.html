Report subject Exploring the Diet and Parasitic Dynamics of Imperial Cormorants (Leucocarbo atriceps) in the Falkland Islands

Abstract

Understanding prey-predator interactions is crucial for comprehending animal behavior, population dynamics, and the repercussions of human activities and climate change on ecosystems, especially in marine environments. Seabirds, as top predators, offer valuable insights into these dynamics, with the Imperial Cormorant being a notable example found in South American waters, particularly in Chile, Argentina, and the Falkland Islands. Despite this, Falkland Islands populations remain relatively understudied. This study aims to enhance our understanding of the Imperial Cormorant's diet while also providing the first description of endoparasites present in the Falkland Islands area. Pellet analysis was chosen as the method due to its non-invasive nature and ease of access. Initial findings indicate that the species predominantly feeds on fish, followed by crustaceans and cephalopods. Regarding endoparasites, nematode larvae (Anisakids) were most prevalent, with trematode eggs (Heterophyidae) also being identified. Attention will be paid to assessing consistency in diet and parasite presence across seasons. Furthermore, focus will be directed towards prey-parasite relationships to gain deeper insights into parasite circulation within the ecosystem.

Report subject Study of temporal variations in wintering shorebird numbers in relation to benthic macrofauna in Aiguillon Bay between 2004 and 2014

Abstract

Shorebird populations are declining worldwide. Aiguillon Bay is an important wintering area for shorebirds. During this overwintering period, waders feed on the benthic macrofauna present on the mudflats to acquire sufficient energy resources. Knowledge of available trophic resources and their variability is therefore essential. This is why benthic macrofauna monitoring has been in place since 2004 in various areas of the Pertuis Charentais. The results show the dominance of the gastropod P. ulvae. Benthic macrofauna is subject to inter-annual variations specific to each species. These variations can be explained by a number of factors, including temperature, salinity and recruitment. The relationship between food resources and shorebird numbers has not been demonstrated. However, a trend of decreasing or increasing numbers with the abundance of certain species can be viewed in 2009 and 2011. The varied diet of shorebirds may explain why variations in prey abundance do not influence numbers. Food resources are not the only factor to consider. Habitat loss or pollution are factors that could explain these variations. Studies on water quality would be of interest to understand the potential threats this may have on benthic macrofauna and therefore on shorebirds.

Report subject Study of the trophic structure of the Bay of Biscay through taxa, bentho‐demersal and pelagic compartments, and the size of marine species with the use of stable isotopes of carbon and nitrogen.

Abstract

Stable isotopes are widely used in the study of the trophic ecology of marine organisms. This study was carried out in connection with the OSPAR international convention, which aims to protect the marine environment of the North-East Atlantic. The aim of this report was to study the trophic structure of the Bay of Biscay (North-East Atlantic Ocean) in terms of taxa, benthic-demersal and pelagic compartments, and species size. To this end, data on several species were collected from various publications. The results showed that there are more trophic levels in the pelagic compartment than in the benthic-demersal compartment. At taxa level, a diversity of food sources and trophic levels was shown. The trophic structure of the pelagic compartment has the same pelagic food source, with a food web structure and a main energy transfer pathway. The fish and crustaceans in this compartment are taxa with the same structure. On the other hand, the bentho-demersal compartment has a variety of food sources. However, bentho-demersal fish have a trophic structure with the same bentho-demersal food source. The increase in species size leads to an increase in the trophic level in the pelagic and benthic-demersal compartments. At the taxa level, this has been observed in pelagic fish and bentho-demersal crustaceans. However, this increase in size only had an effect on the food source in the pelagic com- partment, even though there is a great deal of uncertainty regarding the model. At the taxa level, a diversity of food sources was observed in bentho-demersal crustaceans and pelagic and bentho-demersal fish. Establishing the trophic structure of the Bay of Biscay will therefore enable protection and conservation plans to be put in place for this part of the North-East Atlantic.
